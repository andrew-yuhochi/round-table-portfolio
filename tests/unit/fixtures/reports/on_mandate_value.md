BRK, JNJ, and LMT are trading at discounts to conservative intrinsic value estimates, offering a meaningful margin of safety at current prices. The thesis rests on durable competitive moats, strong free-cash-flow generation, and balance sheets that can weather a mild recession without diluting shareholders.

BRK (Berkshire Hathaway): The stock trades at approximately 1.3× P/B, below its 10-year median of 1.5×. Owner earnings (operating earnings less maintenance capex) yield roughly 6.5% at current prices — a compelling FCF yield for a business of this quality. Net debt is effectively negative given the insurance float; interest coverage is not a concern. The margin of safety here is genuine: paying below 1.4× book for a collection of high-quality businesses with durable competitive moats.

JNJ (Johnson & Johnson): Normalized P/E of 14× on through-cycle earnings implies a meaningful discount to intrinsic value relative to peers. FCF yield of approximately 5.5% supports the dividend and buyback without straining the balance sheet. Earnings quality is high: cash conversion has averaged above 90% over the past five years. The litigation tail is acknowledged but the balance sheet (AA-rated) provides ample coverage for worst-case settlements.

LMT (Lockheed Martin): P/E of 16× on consensus forward earnings is reasonable for a defense franchise with a 6-year backlog. FCF yield of ~6% funds both dividend growth and buybacks. The moat is the F-35 program lock-in and long-cycle government contracting — hard to displace.
